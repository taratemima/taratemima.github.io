Sleeping Around (That Voice Again)?

by Tara Edwards

"Still I hear that sharp tongue talking, Talking tangled words.
I can sense the danger--just listen to the wind."

"I don't sleep around." Who said it and why and what in response to doesn't matter now, but the words still sting. Never mind that I was straightforward about everything that was going on, including the fact that I was involved with another guy. I wanted to tell him "I don't," but I didn't have the words to explain to him the difference between sleeping with others and sleeping around. Then again, I don't think he wanted to hear it.

"I want to be with you, I want to be clear But each time I try its the voice I hear
Hear that voice again."

Now I reminded of that accusation again. I met a guy over Labor Day. Funny, sweet, musical, really attracted to me (and I to him)--and oh, in a monogamous relationship. Okay, so I'll just get to know him better as friend.

"Oh, I'm hearing right and wrong so clearly There's must be more than this."

Sooner or later, as we get to know each other as friends, my mildly interesting love life will come up. So will the p-word: polyamory. I have hinted at it, but not out right said it to him. It's not a word in Websters, and it's not something everyone can get into. That's fine.

"I want you close, I want you near, I cant help but listen but I dont want to hear
Hear that voice again."

What worries me is that the same accusation of sleeping around will come up. The only time I really consider myself sleeping around was when I was 18 and slept with three people--one I didn't want to be with, two I wanted to be with more than one night, but who didnt feel that way. It wasn't the casual sex that made it sleeping around in the worst sense. I was not emotionally connected with these people. I felt afraid to tell them what I really wanted and bowed to their whims, even when it meant I wasn't going to see or speak to them. I didn't trust them. Unfortunately, in most people's minds, like Mr."I don't sleep around," having more than one partner is sleeping around. That automatically makes you shallow, immature, and a slut. Someone once called poly relationships a cop-out--considering all the thinking I've done about the people I was involved with, all the emotion, that really hurt.

"I can get so scared . . ."

I don't know what this guy will think. Or maybe that's not the problem. I know that I can be needy, lonely, and look for attention from others to fill it. I doubt, however, that my personal hurt will automagically be solved by having only one partner. Or even if all my problems were solved I would automagically look for only one partner. I also refuse to believe that being polyamorous means that I'm shallow and immature. I may be pretty young, but I know that maturity has nothing to do with age--or relationship style.

So, I talked myself into the answer. If the Labor Day guy ever asks about what poly means, I will tell him. He doesn't have to consider himself that (it's one hell of a topic to broached to your long time SO), he can just be my friend, I just want him to consider that what I do is more complex than sleeping around.

"What I carry in my heart, brings us so close or so far apart.
Only love can make love."

Copious quotes from Peter Gabriel's "That Voice Again."
