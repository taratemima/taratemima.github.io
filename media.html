If we weren't impeccable libertarians, we'd be hollering for legislation to require media to publish a what-we-really-meant-to-say paragraph on anything having to do with culture or society, to explain why the Internet-as-addictive-scourge story and similar tales routinely arise. Such a policy might actually be of service to anxious news consumers, who are barraged daily with warnings about the perils of TV, computers, rap music, coffee, sex, cars, crime, the educational system, food additives and colorings, artificial sweeteners, and children's toys, clothes, and books.

Media Rant's proposed truth-in-media paragraph would read this way:

"We know this story is absurd, and if you think about it for five seconds, you'll think so, too. But we seem to have lost the ability as a medium to cover the reality of change and to provide a sense of perspective and proportion to the various dangers facing us and our children. We do this because we have become increasingly lazy, unwilling to figure out the truth ourselves, and more and more reliant on polls, spokespeople, academics, and therapists whose livelihood, funding, and place in society often depends on their interests being perceived as urgent and timely. Reporting on these doomsday scenarios makes us feel relevant and useful: life is dangerously unpredictable, but we'll keep you safe if you read our perpetual warnings - and thus you won't forsake us for all this glitzy new online stuff. We continue in this manner even as the economics and demographics of our industry make it clear with each passing day that we are becoming useless, irrelevant, and mistrusted precisely because of dumb reporting like this."
