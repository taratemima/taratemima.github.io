Leviathan, behemoth, Goliath, philistine, hallelujah, jubilee, jeremiad, amen, messiah, cherub, seraphic, Sabbath, Solomonic, satanic, hosanna, Armageddon are words one Forward columnist put as Hebrew words from the Bible. So did, however, emerald, elephant, Levite, and angel. Some common English idioms were originally translated from the Hebrew, such 'put an end to' and 'to pour one's heart out.' The Bible isn't the only source for Hebrew words. Think of rabbinical Judaism-Mishnah, chuppah-or Israel-kibbutz, kippah. Looking at Hebrew-derived words, one can trace the course of Biblical translation, English Jewish populations, and the formation of the State of Israel. A definite sea change took place in Hebrew-derived words, not only were they straight from Hebrew and not by the route of Greek, Latin, and Arabic, but also moved from different religious and secular concerns, from Christianity to science, to rabbinical Judaism to world politics. This is a small attempt to tell a brief history of these developments.

 

Nearly all of the Hebrew words from the Bible fall into three categories, according to the Forward article (p.10). The first one are words coined from Hebrew names but having an independent meaning of their own, taking it from the biblical narrative surrounding the word. This includes Goliath, philistine, jeremiad and Solomonic. Modern Hebrew has some words that fall into that, such as Hadassah.

 

The second category of Hebrew words from the Bible is words whose exact meaning was not known to the Bible's translators, so they left it in the original rather than have to attempt a guess. Among those words are leviathan (Modern Hebrew means "whale"), cherub, and seraph.

 

The third category are words whose meaning was understood by translators, but had no adequate term to render it. The most famous is Sabbath, and its different derivations in English are discussed later. Many Judaism-specific terms outside of the Bible fall into that category, too. In recent years, the Hebrew original is winning over the English translation, with 'Yom Kippur' being preferred over 'Day of Atonement,' and 'tefillim' being preferred to 'prayer.' One such word, 'tzedekeh,' will be discussed later.

 

Most of the Hebrew loanwords in English first were Greek and Latin borrowings, due to the influence of the Septuagint version of the Bible, then later the Vulgate. What Old English translations existed were psalters, hymns, and religious instruction for churchgoers and monks. Some very common, Christianity-related words came from these Hebrew-through-Greco-Latin borrowings.

 

Satan is Latin, then Greek, derived from 'sātān,' Hebrew for 'adversary, one who plots against another,' from 'sātan,' the verb infinitive 'to plot, to oppose.' According to the OED, "In the Old Testament the Heb. word ordinarily denotes a human adversary, but in some of the later portions (Job, Chronicles, Zechariah, and Psalms) it occurs (chiefly with definite article) [it occurs] as the designation of an angelic being hostile to mankind, who tempts men to evil and accuses them to God."

 

Myrrh was from the Greek 'μύρρα,' derived from the Hebrew 'mōr,' and entered English around 825 C.E., from a Vespers translation of Psalms. The meaning of the fragrant gum and products from it remain consistant.

Angel is, according to the OED, an early Teutonic (Gothic, too) adaptation of "the L[atin] angel-us, or G[reek] 'ăγγέλοs' - 'a messenger,' used by the LXX to translate Hebrew 'mal'āk,' in full

'mal'āk-yĕhōwāh' 'messenger of Jehovah'; whence the name and doctrine of angels passed into Latin and the modern languages." The Lindisf translation first put the word in English language writing.

 

Amen is, according to the OED, a. Latin 'āmēn,' apparently from the Hebrew word 'āmēn,' 'certainty, truth,' "from the Hebrew verb āman to strengthen, confirm; used adverbially 'certainly, verily, surely' as an expression of affirmation, consent, or ratification of what has been said by another (Deut. xxvii.

26, 1 Kings i. 36); adopted in Greek by the LXX., whence in New Testament, and in early Christian use, in Greek and Latin, as a solemn expression of belief, affirmation, consent, concurrence, or ratification, of any formal utterance made by a representative; thus with prayers, imprecations, confessions of faith. Apparently not so used in Old English, but translated by 'Sóðlice!,' 'Swá hit ys' or 'sý!' Added however as a concluding formula to Luke and John in the Augustine Gospels." First recorded use was the Linsdisfell Gospel.

 

Aloe was the English translation of the Hebrew 'akhālīm' and 'akhālōth' (incidentally coming from Sanskrit 'aguru') for the fragrant resin of the aloe plant, originally found in the Septuagint version of the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament. Aloe was also the English translation for the Greek word for the bitter drug from that same plant, 'άλόη.'

 

Hell, while originally a Old Norse referring to the goddess of the underworld, was used to translate the Hebrew 'shĕōl,' a word meaning 'pit' and first used in the Vespers Psalter.

 

The earliest English translation of the whole Bible was by William Wyclif in 1362, working from the Vulgate. "William Tyndale, a scholar fluent in 7 languages, left England to work on the first English translation based on the original Hebrew and Greek. In 1525, smuggled copies of his New Testament began circulating England," according to the writers at wycliffe.org. No word on which texts, but could be from the Masoretic Hebrew text and the Septuagint Greek text. Later came the Coverdale, in 1535, then the Geneva in 1560. As for James I, "James I (1564-1625), King of England, alarmed by all the versions appearing, commissioned a group of biblical scholars to produce an authorized version, combining the best of earlier translations. The Authorized Version, written in the language of the day, appeared in 1611, the first Bible produced by an authorized group of scholars," according to wycliffe.org. Along with Greek scholars, some of England's major Hebraists were on that panel. Each translation added new words.

 

What is most interesting about the new words is that they were not religious in nature, but refer to natural phenomena. Also, travel, medical, law, and mystical texts first introduced some of the new words, not just translations of the Bible. By the time King James commissioned the translation, there was a strong Christian Hebraist community in Europe and in England, willing to read ancient and rabbinical Jewish texts. Some of these words refer to the mystical oral tradition of the Torah, called the Kabalah. They were willing to look at words and concepts not found in the Psalters, even as some of them denigrated their source.

 

English language and English identity really developed in the seventeenth century. As more major English-language works were published and read, "Biblical paradigms increasingly overwhelmed classical and folk models (Shoulson, 355)." In religion, "[English] Protestantism . . .was greatly influenced by an evolving extra-biblical Hebraism, one that extended beyond an interest in the Hebrew of the Bible to include the writings of Jewish scholars of the ancient and medieval past (Shoulson, 355)." Attitudes concerning the authors of the works, of course, varied. They tended toward either respect for scholarship by the far-flung, close-knit Contential Hebraists of the late sixteenth and early seventeenth century, to the rants of George Withers, translator of a 'metrical Psalms, about the perversity and stubbornness of the Jewish people, to the image of England as a 'spiritual Israel,' heirs of the Bible, whose language is best suited for universial understanding and adherence to the Biblical authors' themes. Consulting rabbis about points of Hebrew grammar, definitions of faith, and opinions on the New Testament did not just result in the Bible translations English-speaking people still use. That period "culminat[ed] in the Whitehall Conference of 1655-56 and the deliberation over the readmission of the Jews to England (Shoulman, 355)."

 

Much of the new words from Tynsdale, King James, and individual translations were nature related, using more words from the Bible to describe lands beyond the island. A small number will be examined here. Elephant is found in the Celtic languages as a corrupted form of Latin, then Greek. Some speculate that it was originally from the Hebrew 'eleph,' meaning 'ox.' Emerald was actually Greek, and used to translate the Hebrew 'nōphek,' rendered in the Vulgate as 'carbunculus.' Cinnamon was originally Latin, derived from the Greek, which itself was derived from the Hebrew 'qinnāmōn,' found approximately 1430 in a book called Bochas.

 

Alcohol was first written in various travel and scientific books, and had come to mean two things. It means either "The fine metallic powder used in the East to stain the eyelids, etc.: powdered ore of antimony, stibnite, or antimony trisulphide" or "By extension to fluids of the idea of sublimation (of extracting powder): An essence, quintessence, or 'spirit,' obtained by distillation or 'rectification.'"

The words itself is the French spelling of the Latin word, derived from the Arabic word 'al-koh'l,' used for a fine powder to stain the eyelids. The Arabic word itself comes from the Hebrew 'kahala,' from the root 'kahal,' 'to stain.'

 

Cabal was a French and Spanish writing of 'cabbala,' which itself is the Latin writing of the Hebrew 'qabbālāh,' meaning 'tradition,' from the root, 'qibbēl,' 'to receive.' While the word 'cabal' once referred to "the Jewish tradition as to the interpretation of the Old Testament" (which is in PDE 'Kabalah'), 'cabal' now means "secret or private intrigue of a sinister character formed by a small body of persons," which entered around 1646. 'Cabbala' entered around 1521, and 'cabal' followed in 1611.

 

After re-admittance in 1695, the words came in hard and fast, most from texts explaining the Jews, and others from Jewish religious works in English.

 

In Purchas' Pilgrimage, he makes many references to different Jewish practices, introducing into English words like tefillin, Midrash, and tallith (now tallis). E. Chilmead traditionally wrote three or so volumes of Leon Modena's Historical Rites, Customes, Etc. of Jews in 1650 and introduced into the English language chazzan (commonly cantor is used in lieu of that), hesped, megillah (Hebrew, 'roll' or 'scroll'), mezuzah, and mitzvah.

 

As noted, the study of the Kabalah brought in some words. Mecubalist came from the same root as 'cabbala,' only now written in Hebrew as 'mequbbāl,' 'one who receives,' with the English suffix -ist added. It is only found in one text, a treatise on physiognomy in 1653. The few times I do see the word is in English language works on the Kabalah, and it is spelt 'mekubalist.' Shekinah is found in religious works, both Jewish and Christian (who used it to refer to Jesus) to refer to the presence of God, with the cherubim or in any holy place, and is Hebrew, from the root 'shākan,' 'to dwell, to rest.' Zohar means literally, 'light, splendor,' and is the title of a major Kabbalistic text, in the form of an allegorical interpretation of the Pentateuch. It first entered in 1682, spelt 'zoar.' Gematria seems to be itself a Greek loanword, from 'γέωμέτρίά,' our source for the word geometry. Gematria is a method of interpreting the Hebrew Scriptures by interchanging words whose letters has the same numerical value when added. It is first used in Goad's Celestial Bodies. Cardimelech is specifically mystical concept in early medicine, 'a supposed active principle in the heart, superintending what are now called the vital functions,' according to the OED. It is a Greco-Hebrew hybrid loanword, adding the Greek word for 'heart' and the Hebrew word for 'king.'

 

English Jews assimilated fast into the culture, and found most of their identity and education in English. As generations grow up, more of them lose their multilingual orientation and write and speak solely in English. Meanwhile, with the Enlightenment in England, scientific knowledge and skepticism found common ground in Anglician erudition, along with continued study of Hebrew from the 16th century to the 18th. When Anglician theologians and Hebraists propose newer, better, more Christianized translations of Hebrew Bible texts, the leading lights of the English Jewish community fight back. While the English Christians have gotten familiar with Hebrew and woven it with their national and religious identity, the English Jews have done the same with English. Perhaps this is linked to Hasaklah and Geiger's Reform Judaism in Germany; perhaps it is their unique response to the 'clergymen-scientists' of England.

 

The focus on Bishop Robert Lowth and David Levi is not accidental. In addition to writing a dictionary and rules of prescriptive English grammar called Short Introduction to English Grammar (1762), Lowth was also a predominant Anglican clergyman. He set up another quest for the English language when he translated the Book of Isaiah, in 1778. Remember that Isaiah is an important prophetic work, from which Christians get a good part of the messianic prophecies that they believed are fulfilled by Jesus. The translation was as much a matter of national identity and education as his rules of grammar, but it was a lot more:

 

Lowth had established the full significance of his project from the first page of his handsome volume, dedicated to the King of England, as a part of 'Sacred Criticism, which to the honour of this Nation, and to the Universal benefit of the Christian Church . . .' the bishop's crusade on the behalf of a new Christian text of the Old Testament, both artistically and religiously superior to the older version taken from the Jews, was no small matter to be taken lightly. Lowth's "declaration of independence" accomplished by the weight of the full Christian political and religious establishment, was meant to undermine Jewish claims of being the sole arbiters of the test (Ruderman, 76).

 

David Levi was not a clergy member, but he was an English Jew who consciously wrote for Christians, deists, and Jews in the English language. He is not very well known, because writing solely in English limited his audience in the 18th century. A hat maker, he was also accomplished in Hebrew and a great reader of Christian Biblical scholarship and history. "Levi assigned himself the formidable tasks of becoming Anglo-Jewry's primary educator as well as its principal apologist and defender against the verbal assaults of Christian theologians, biblical critics, Evangelicals, deists, and atheists. He was a one-man anti-defamation league and his list of publications constituted a virtual Judaic library in their own right (Ruderman, 57)." Lowth's translation was especially important-"in the new environment of Christian Hebraism, the notion that a Christian, especially one with the credentials of the erudite Lowth, could understand the Hebrew text and translate it more effectively and more poetically than any contempory Jew was surely a painful pill for so proud a Jew as Levi to swallow (Ruderman, 76)." He not only by writing Dissertations on the Prophecies of the Old Testament, taking the bishop to task for inaccurate statements about Jewish beliefs and rituals, mistakes of the Hebrew idiom, and whole idea of collation (comparing different manuscripts and debating which verse was oldest and truest) and translation.

 

In one thing, Levi and Lowth were alike. Lowth saw a pressing need to translate the Bible into English for enlightenment of the English-speaking Christian who knew very little to none Latin or Hebrew. Levi saw a pressing need to write Jewish religious education material in English for the enlightenment of the English-speaking Jew who knew very little Hebrew. Among other things, Levi wrote A Succinct Account of the Duties, Rites, and Ceremonies of the Jews, intended for children and adults wanting to know more and correct misunderstandings. Around that time, more English-language treatises, instructions for ritual, and books on ethics and morality, were published, with even rival Hebrew Bible translations!

 

In America, the Jewish settlers were also becoming predominately English speakers, and they use Jewish writings in the English language. Many of Levi's writings were reprinted in cities like Philadelphia, and 19th century Jewish educators used the same materials English Jewish educators used. The influence did not stop at books. For any and all attempts to establish a different hegemony, like some German-born community leaders and rabbis in 19th century America, these attempts did not take root. "In a community where rabbis played a relatively diminished role in shaping Jewish communal priorities, where the intellectual legacy of European Jews . . .was hardly meaningful for most Jews, and where the Jewish laity was asserting its right to adapt the tradition as it saw fit, the American Jewish community looked more and more like its English counterpart than its German one (Ruderman, 272)."

 

Because the translators felt there were few equivalents in the English language for many Jewish concepts in Hebrew, they left them as is, often with English definitions. This resulted in another explosion of Hebrew loanwords.

 

The word chasid itself is an interesting doublet. In Wycliff's translation of the Bible, 'assidian' was the name given to the followers of the Macabees, who "defended the purity of their worship against the attempts of Antiochus Epiphanes to introduce idolatry," according to the OED. It was later used in 1834 to refer to members of a Jewish sect seeking an intimate connection with God. PDE now knows them as chasids or 'chasidim.' Both came from the Hebrew 'hăsīdīm,' meaning 'holy ones, saints.'

 

Beth Din is Hebrew, meaning 'house of judgment.' Interesting enough, the OED has quotations from a law book and two newspapers ("Should the proceedings of London's Jewish House of Judgment--the Beth-Din--be kept secret," asked the Sunday Dispatch in 1928).

 

I cannot attempt a brief history of Yiddish, nor will I try. It is sufficient to say Yiddishisms entered the English language, mostly among Jewish immigrants to America from Eastern Europe, but also to England. Most of them have either Hebrew or German roots, but some are from the Slavic languages.

The Oxford English Dictionary says mamzer was actually "adopted by the Vulgate in Deut. xxiii. 2 (where it appears with the gloss 'id est de scorto natus'), and hence frequently used in the Middle Ages." It entered Yiddish, and re-entered the English language as a slang term for "one who borrows frequently, or who expects much attention and many favors; a sponger... Any disliked person; a bastard," according to Wentworth in 1960. Rosten has it as "often used to describe a very bright child, a clever or ingenious person, a resourceful, gets-things-done, corner-cutting type" in 1968.

 

Shamash (from the Hebrew root 'shimmesh,' 'to serve') did enter in 1650, along with Chilmead's other words. However, it is best known as from the Yiddish 'shames.' That word appears in not only Judaism-specific writings, but also religious texts. Tochus is slang for the backside, and is originally from Yiddish, from the Hebrew root 'tahat,' 'underneath.' Shidduch is also Yiddish from Hebrew (root 'shidduk,' 'courtship, arranged marriage').

 

Meshuga is Yiddish, and has its roots in Hebrew and German words. The Hebrew 'mĕshuggā'' is a particle of 'shāgag', meaning 'to go astray, wander,' and is also related to the German meschugge meaning 'crazy.'

 

The meaning of schmooze has ameorilized from the Hebrew root, 'shĕmū'ah,' a word meaning 'rumor.' The Hebrew became 'shmues,' 'to chat or gossip,' and generally means a pleasant, long, intimate chat with someone.

 

While Jewish education was written in English, a concurrent revolution sought to make Hebrew a language for secular as well as religious matters. A major text on Israeli history and Hebrew change, Language and Revolution notes five aliyas, the first happening in 1881. With each successive new immigrant to Palestine, new words came in English. Often, these words were not only names of settling organizations, but things not found anywhere else.

 

Yishuv, meaning 'settlement,' was used for Jewish communities in Palestine from the 19th century to 1948.

Poale Zion, meaning 'workers of Zion,' if you have not guessed, is a left-wing Zionist labor organization founded in 1899 in Russia. Hadassah, the Hebrew name of Biblical Esther, "An American Zionist women's organization, founded in 1912, which contributes to welfare work in Israel." Sabra is a native Israeli, from the Hebrew word for a prickly pear. Sabra is one of the many English words that come from a metaphor. Not surprisingly, it appeared around 1948. Pita is Greek, Turkish, and Hebrew and different variants appeared around the same time. The Greek variant meant 'bread, cake, or pie,' but the meaning has narrowed to thick and flat bread found in Mediterranean and Middle Eastern foods. Aliyah was used in 1933 in the group Youth Aliyah, for the emigration of young Jews to Palestine, and then to any travel or move to Israel (from Hebrew, 'ascent'). Kibbutz is a Modern Hebrew word for 'gathering,' and its meaning has narrowed to mean a large farming collective.

 

I will examine the words that had recently entered English, and implications. The three words I found had to do with three different trends: Liberal Judaism delving into more specific rather than universal concepts and forming new rituals; Traditional Judaism asserting itself through its own writings; and interchange and dialogue between the two. A history of denominations of American Judaism would take a paper in its self, but the discussion on just what is religion (especially with most Christian scholarship focusing on understanding of shared tradition rather than supersession) supposed to do has contributed so new loanwords, and helped push more of the Hebrew word or phrase being preferred over the English.

 

Shabbat is the Sephardic pronunciation and spelling of 'sabbat.' While 'sabbath' (used instead of 'dias Saturni' in Bible translation) entered the language much sooner, when Sephardic pronunciation of Hebrew became the norm for secular and religious works, Shabbat was used more in Judaism-specific writings. It first entered in 1934. Shabbos is the Yiddish spelling and pronunciation.

 

Bat Mitzvah ('daughter of the covenant') was based on Bar Mitzvah ('son of the covenant'), and if I remember right, was introduced by Mordecai Kaplan for Reform congregations. The word itself entered English in 1950.

 

Kippah, a skullcap worn by Orthodox male Jews, and by male Jews on religious occasions, is in semantic competition with the Yiddish word for the head covering, 'yarmulke,' an older word (Yiddish derived from the Polish 'jarmulka'). Kippah entered in 1964, while yarmulke entered 1903.

 

Perhaps the biggest example of a concept that goes from being translated into the English equivalent to having its own word is tzedakah. Before 1959, it would have just been translated into 'charity,' 'righteousness' (the literal translation of the Hebrew) or 'helping other Jews.' Now, the whole word, tzedakah, is used.

 

With all this new information, it is easy to lose prospective of just what these changes are. Let us look at the word the Forward mentioned as a premier Biblical Hebrew loanword, 'Sabbath.' It is also a great example of English language change brought on by Yiddish and Modern Hebrew. Shabbat is the Sephardic pronunciation and spelling of 'sabbat.' While 'sabbath' (used instead of 'dias Saturni' in Bible translation) entered the language much sooner, when Sephardic pronunciation of Hebrew became the norm for secular and religious works, Shabbat was used more in Judaism-specific writings, while text for Christian or Jewish and Christian audiences use Sabbath. It first entered in 1934. Shabbos is the Yiddish spelling and pronunciation.

 

For now, it seems that Hebrew loanwords have stopped coming into the English language. Perhaps, with changes in Jewish religious practice (for instance, outreach to people interested in converting and interest in adoption and in-vitro fertilization) and in Israeli politics (the recent 'troubles') may introduce new loanwords to better describe the situation. Maybe also, other languages will adopt Hebrew loanwords and introduce them into English by that route. Time, and a new version of the OED, will tell.
