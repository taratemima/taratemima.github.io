Hi Tom... thanks for the info on The Good Book which interprets the bible in a more humane and homophilic way.

I've been staging a series of weekly confrontations with evangelical types at the University of Maine at Farmington.

For the past several years, a very small cult calling themselves "Friends of Christ" have been coming to the campus on whichever night the L/B/G/T support group meets. Truthfully, there are only four people in the cult, and just two of them with huge, bellowing voices come out each week recruiting our students. Because they stand on the street, which is a public thoroughfare, they are protected by their first amendment rights. Even downtown merchants have tried to have them stopped from screaming at shoppers on Saturdays, but to no legal avail.

The style of preaching is obnoxious... The couple dresses in grey wool cloaks, somewhat similar to the kinds that the Puritans or Quakers might have worn. Locally, they are known as "The Grey People." Although they yell derogatory and defaming allegations at all students (we are all burning in hell for our original and not-so-creative sins), one of the faves is condemnation of homosexuality. One year they were even allowed to rent a table in the student center, just like any other vendors, and displayed a huge banner claiming that "Homosexuality is an abomination."

I've been wanting to do something about this for a long time, as many students have taken the evangelists' warnings personally, (especially women who've been sexually molested and then told that they are whores for wearing shorts).

Now, each week when the weather is dry, I throw on an elegant black cape that I found at an antique shop, and with my bullhorn tucked inside the cape, I head out onto the street to begin exercising MY first amendment rights. The first time I did it, there were not many people outside at all... but just as I was wrapping up my "dress rehearsal" and making notes for improving my shtick, a confluence of students emerging from the dining hall and others leaving their classes formed a crowd of about 200... and so I began reading again from a prepared statement on first amendment rights, human dignity, and finished with excerpts from Michelangelo Signorile's Queer Manifesto.

The second week, many professors brought their classes outside to watch and listen to the this street theatre, and in following weeks I've been asked to address a variety of different classes to discuss gay rights, gay marriage, and the university's new domestic partnership policy.

My goal with all of this is not to directly challenge the evangelists' interpretations of the Bible... I don't expect to make a dent in their beliefs. I'm hoping to create some discussion of differing religious views, and certainly to encourage more discussion of gay issues. So far that's worked very well.

Another goal is to encourage the evangelists to speak at a lower volume, and to voice the loving rather than condemning and vengeful aspects of God. So far this seems to be happening also.

A third goal of making a public spectacle of myself on the street is to let students have a reality check... until now, people scurry past the preachers and say nothing to the insults and threats hurled at them. My presence makes it clear that at least one adult in the community disagrees with the message and disapproves of the delivery of it.

I used October to stage my own presentation for Gay History Month by including information from the Alyson Almanac on prominent historical figures who had ties to the sexual minorities.

Handouts that I prepared for these events included the Lesbian/Gay/Bisexual Students' Bill of Educational Rights (from Project 21), the Questionnaire for Heterosexuals, and placards to hold reading "Preach, Don't Screech" and "What Would Jesus Say?"

So far I've gotten mail and phone calls from students, faculty, and community members who are supportive of the dialogue I've created. I've been painstakingly careful to assure students that I respect the evangelists for the conviction of their faith that results in their persistence in the face of humility and such... In turn, even the many students who think it's funny or weird that I'm out there as the "Caped Avenger" have remembered my sound bites about respect for others' views. I've overheard many of them talking about how weird the whole thing is, but they end up agreeing that there isn't anything especially wrong with homosexuality.

With respect to The Good Book, there have been a flurry of calls from Farmington area supporters who want me to come up with "evidence" from the bible that they can use in defending gays at church discussions. With the advice that faith is a hard nut to crack, I offer some examples of these biblical references, and remind these callers that the most they should expect to accomplish is dialogue, not conversion. Now I'll have another resource to turn them on to!
