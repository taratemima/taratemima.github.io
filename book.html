I was browsing through Buck a Book, and came across one particular book. The cover--a pensive, brooding man with dark hair and kind eyes hovering over a notebook. He was a character in One Life to Live, and this is supposed to be his notebook of thoughts and feelings. I can relate--I carried several versions of such a notebook. In it, some ghostwriter details the romance of "Patrick" and "Marty", and their painful (for all involved) breakup when she married another man.

Let me start this by saying that I hate soap operas. I like overwrought emotions and visual lushness as the next person, but there is something cheap about all this. I am broke, bisexual, polyamorous, alternately masculine and feminine acting, deeply romantic and loyal to more than one lover, and confused--where's my place in this amber-lighted universe?

So it was mainly curiosity that made me pick up this book--and love poetry from the likes of Yeats, Lord Byron, Shakespeare and others.

I got a little shaken by the start. Why does the introduction need to remind the reader that she (I am using that pronoun because the target audience is hetreosexual women) is reading about a scene in Ireland? "Irish whiskey," "Irish peat," some old man named Liam--I know that the Irish are supposed to be maudlin sentimentalists, but even we have our limits. Hell, I slammed down the Vampire Lestat when I found out that the female drummer of Satan's Night Out was named Tough Cookie. One dumbass move, and I'm pissed.

But still I skimmed it, if only to read the poetry. I saw pictures of "Marty," "Patrick," and her soon-to-be husband "Dylan." Something screamed "Hey, "Marty", why not both?" Here are these obivous great guys, different in temperment and dreams, but similiarly good-hearted.

But people in the love triangle seemed happy being monogamous, and the viewers of the soap opera would perfer them monogamous. What does the opinion of a pervert matter?

Besides, the love poems that "Patrick" quoted concentrated on one person. About the only other time when they were written about more than one is when the speaker is a cad stringing along other women. But maybe Paul was right about one thing--love is not selfish and greedy. It loves the whole person, and realizes that the beloved has thoughts, feelings, and experiences not like the lover. Maybe "Patrick" needs to pick up some modern poetry. :)

But still, real life women will be buying this book. Women who are busy working. Women with negiglent, abusive, or absent husbands and boyfriends. Women with whiny kids. Women who watched every episode of One Life to Live just to see this triangle.

The female readers were saying these poems out loud and imagining that "Patrick" is reading them to only them. It made me pissed for some reason. People are isolated by physical distance and the emotional distance helped by television. To cure this, some turn to television-generated fantasies for their emotional hunger. And the cycle goes on. I am not saying that television is the only cause--but it helps.

It made my heart ache for these women--I can't say why. It's more than sympathy. Parents, books, movies, television make it seem like your life is in preparation for this one man that will be your everything and will make you whole. No one can fulfill all your desires, and only you can make yourself whole. How could they just give in and believe all this crap?

I don't pretend to know everything--but I know that there is something wrong with that picture. It doesn't have to be that way. My lovers and mainly friends are proof.

I guess it is my duty as a weirdo to laugh at these women glued to their TV and holding back tears of longing. But I'm not stupid. There but by the grace of "Bob" goes I.
