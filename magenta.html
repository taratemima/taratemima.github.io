Magenta and Yellow

There he was, coming on from Park Street. Bright magenta shirt and yellow pants, carrying a book called "Gigolo." I could have giggled. Did he think he was one?

But really, how could I care? Things were depressing me, or at least people were. I have a crush on five people, three I saw that night. One I visited in the MITSFS room. I hugged him and scratched his head. I couldn't find the words to tell him how bad I feel when he is not around or that he is not dating me anymore, how I don't know what's up. The second I saw at Preacherfest, in arm-length gloves and a black dress with spaghetti straps. She hasn't been at SIPB for a week. I couldn't tell her that she is the only reason why I'm not studying the inessential stuff at home--that and $US0.45 cans of soda. She left with some kids--I couldn't tell her how I wish she was with me. The third was drunk. He kissed me, I wondered if he would have kissed me if he was sober. I was in the gloomiest mood possible.

Meanwhile, magenta and yellow guy took off his brown shoes to reveal green-and-red socks. He laughed loudly sometimes. I pretended not to notice what he was doing.

Time passed and I got on my stuff. He apparently noticed me. He also noticed my notebook. "What are you writing, bad poetry?" he said in a even voice. Is that supposed to be a pick-up line? I wanted to say "What are drinking, cheap liquor?" But I figured that it would be best to leave him alone.

When no (verbal) response came, he proclaimed "You seem deeply neurotic." Why, thank you, Herr Doktor Freud! *You* certainly feel mentally troubled if you have been the recipent of flirts, then treated just as a friend, or even ignored. I would say that doubt and confusion *would* be troubling. Then again, why should a man who is drunk, has sensationalist tastes in literature, and has no concept of proportion in colors tell me about *my* mental state?

I, again, said nothing, and got off the train. He got off there too, and got on a bus. As I saw the bus pass by, I stuck out my tongue. But he didn't notice.

Tara Edwards, 1995
